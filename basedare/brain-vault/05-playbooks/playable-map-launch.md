# Playable Map Alpha Launch

## Objective

Prove that a newcomer can open BaseDare, understand it without explanation, find one worthwhile nearby action, and participate without the founder operating locally.

The first growth target is not “creator signups.” It is **first useful map action**.

## Consumer promise

> Open the map. Find something to do. Join the local story.

Supporting language:

- Discover real places through what people actually did there.
- Join free challenges, social activities and local routes.
- Build points and reputation; some challenges also pay.
- Explore before signing in.

Avoid leading with:

- creator applications
- Web3, escrow or smart-contract mechanics
- verified arrivals or measurable ROI
- Shadow Army, grid ownership or land-grab language
- venue or brand operating terminology

These concepts can appear after the user chooses an action that needs them.

## Launch zone

Start with one dense, walkable micro-area rather than an empty global map. Siargao is one valid alpha candidate, but the playbook works in any tourist or social district.

Minimum playable inventory:

- 10–12 free Community Sparks
- 3–4 safe public social activities or Drops
- 3–4 small rewarded Dares
- 2 short discovery Routes
- 12+ places with recent, understandable memory

Every item must answer one of four intents: **explore, play, meet, earn**.

## Intent before recommendation

The “What would make your next two hours better?” question is a consent boundary,
not decorative onboarding. Before a visitor answers, the map may show neutral
categories, live counts, and objects, but it must not name a “best next” venue,
push a first-proof location, or auto-open a nearby recommendation.

After the visitor chooses an intent — including an explicit “See what’s on
tonight” tap — BaseDare may rank and name relevant places or activities. Free
roam must remain a real no-recommendation path. A pre-intent paid placement is a
future exception only when it is explicitly funded, clearly labelled
**Promoted**, frequency-capped, and never presented as PeeBear’s organic advice.

## First cohorts

Do not recruit a single generic “creator” cohort. Recruit small groups with distinct reasons to care:

1. **New arrivals and tourists** — need something to do today.
2. **Locals and regulars** — want to share spots and shape local culture.
3. **Social connectors** — already organize surf, fitness, food, music, hostel or coworking groups.
4. **Reward seekers** — willing to complete useful verified actions for small payouts.

Anyone may become a trusted Scout or Host later through completed actions and reputation. Do not make the title the entry gate.

## Four-week loop

### Week 0 — make it understandable

- Route the global Join action to a universal start page.
- Replace creator-first and crypto-first navigation language with ordinary verbs.
- Ensure a visitor can browse the map without a wallet.
- Seed the launch inventory and remove obviously fake/demo money signals.

### Week 1 — private playable alpha

- Invite 20–30 people across the four cohorts.
- Give each person one direct map link and one recommended first action.
- Observe where they hesitate; do not explain unless they are blocked.
- Interview at least five people immediately after their first attempt.

### Week 2 — public daily utility

- Publish “Three things to do today” using real BaseDare map links.
- Share approved completion receipts and place changes, not generic promotional graphics.
- Ask every successful participant to bring one person into a specific action.
- Refresh dead or unclear challenges quickly so the map never feels abandoned.

### Week 3 — social return loop

- Test one public-place group activity with an authorized local host or clearly community-created organizer.
- Notify prior participants when a place they touched gains a new Spark or activity.
- Give repeat participants a visible reputation or route-completion reason to return.

### Week 4 — decide whether density is compounding

- Measure the organic multiplier and participant repeat.
- Remove activity types that produced confusion, safety issues or disposable content.
- Concentrate the next batch around the strongest intent and place cluster.

## Content and outreach

The strongest marketing asset is the live map, not a brand manifesto.

Recurring content formats:

- **Three things to do today** — direct links to live actions.
- **Someone just sparked this place** — one approved mark and why it helps.
- **Can you verify this?** — one small rewarded local question.
- **Weekend route** — a short sequence friends can complete together.
- **What changed here?** — before/after place memory or freshness update.

External outreach remains review-required. Prioritize warm community admins, hostel/coworking groups, activity organizers and small local accounts that can send people into one specific action. Do not send a generic “join our platform” message.

## First-session metrics

Primary funnel:

`map visit -> action opened -> join/claim started -> action completed -> second session`

Initial continuation gates:

- at least 25% of qualified map visitors open an action
- at least 40% of people who start an action complete it
- at least 20% of completers return within 14 days
- at least 20% of completed actions are free/unpaid by the end of the alpha
- zero serious safety incidents or precise-location privacy leaks
- every paid action leaves useful place memory, not only a disposable post

Track separately:

- free Spark completion
- social activity joins
- paid Dare completion
- first-time versus repeat participant
- invited/referral participant
- fully loaded cost per approved paid completion

## Budget discipline

Use a small learning cap before any multi-market tournament:

- rewards and referrals: 40%
- moderation and verification: 25%
- seed content and local distribution: 20%
- contingency and safety: 15%

The initial alpha should fit inside a **$1,000–$1,500 maximum-loss budget**. Spending more is not justified until first-session participation and repeat behavior are measured.

## Safety and operating boundaries

- Never expose exact real-time participant locations publicly.
- Social participation and messaging are opt-in.
- Prefer public places and safe templates.
- BaseDare does not present community-created activities as BaseDare-operated events.
- Locally operated events, perks or commercial promises require an authorized local partner and appropriate compliance.
- Cash, contracts, production admin and external commitments remain human-only.

## Decision rule

Continue when people participate without founder explanation and the map produces repeat or referred activity. Pause and fix the product when growth requires constant manual explanation, paid actions create no unpaid activity, or the map still feels like a static directory.
